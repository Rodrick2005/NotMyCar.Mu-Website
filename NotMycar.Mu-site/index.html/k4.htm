By Drew Dorian

Overview

The K4 is a stylish new compact sedan that replaces the outgoing Forte and slots in under the larger K5 family sedan in Kia's lineup. The K4 wears a fastback roofline that draws inspiration from the Stinger sports sedan and wide rear fenders that give the car a sporty stance. The base engine is the same 147-hp four-cylinder that powered last year's Forte. A 190-hp turbocharged four is exclusive to the top-rung GT-Line Turbo trim. The K4 is slightly larger than the outgoing Forte, and Kia says the interior is roomier, particularly for rear-seat riders, aligning it with other compact sedans such as the Honda Civic, the Mazda 3, the Toyota Corolla, and the Volkswagen Jetta. We will have to wait a bit longer for the handsome hatchback variant, which Kia says will launch in the U.S. later, possibly for the 2026 model year.

What's New for 2025?

The K4 is a new nameplate for Kia in North America and this model replaces the Forte compact car. An electric model called EV4 may also join the lineup later on.

    Engine, Transmission, and Performance

    Most K4 models have a 147-hp 2.0-liter four-cylinder engine, front-wheel drive, and a continuously variable automatic transmission (CVT). When we drove the K4 with this powertrain, we found it offers sluggish acceleration but good refinement. Luckily, Kia is also offering a 190-hp turbo 1.6-liter four as an upgrade engine. The more powerful engine is exclusive to the GT-Line Turbo trim and it comes with a conventional eight-speed automatic. The GT-Line and GT-Line Turbo also come with a more sophisticated multi-link rear suspension, whereas all the other K4s come with a torsion-beam setup. During our drive of the GT-Line Turbo model, we found its handling to be competent but not exciting, as it's not as sporty as rivals such as the Honda Civic and Mazda 3. The turbo engine makes the car reasonably quick, but the transmission's reluctance to downshift means the K4 is not as responsive for passing as we'd like.

    0-60MPH Times

    At our test track, the GT-Line Turbo model got to 60 mph in 7.3 seconds. We haven't yet tested the base engine but we'll update this section when we do.

    Fuel Economy and Real-World MPG

    With the base four-cylinder under its hood, the Kia K4 earns 30 mpg city and 40 mpg highway ratings from the EPA. That puts it squarely in the middle of the fuel economy bracket that includes competitors like the Honda Civic and Mazda 3. The peppier GT-Line Turbo has slightly lower ratings of 26 mpg city and 36 mpg highway. On our 75-mph highway fuel economy route, the GT-Line Turbo achieved 35 mpg. For more information about the Kia K4s fuel economy, visit the EPA's website.

      Interior, Comfort, and Cargo

      Kia has kept the K4's interior design clean and simple, with a linear look to the dash. Although there's a large infotainment display, there are also still several physical buttons for controls, which is refreshing given the auto industry's push to digitize such things. Unfortunately, the available touchscreen that operates the climate controls is easily blocked by the steering wheel. At least the wheel itself is interesting, as it looks different from the wheel in other Kia models and features an offset logo.

      Infotainment and Connectivity

        The K4's standard infotainment setup includes a 12.3-inch screen, and upper trims come with a multi-screen arrangement that sees two 12.3-inch displays combined in one continuous flat panel. One serves as a configurable gauge cluster while the other does duty as the main point of access for features such as navigation, radio, climate control, and more. A third, 5.0-inch display sits between the two larger ones and provides access to climate controls. Wireless Apple CarPlay and Android Auto are both standard; SiriusXM satellite radio and in-dash navigation will also likely be available across the K4's lineup.

        Safety and Driver-Assistance Features

        A suite of driver-assistance features is standard on all K4 models, but more advanced tech, including an evasive steering assist feature and a more advanced adaptive cruise control system with lane centering, are optional. For more information about the K4's crash-test results, visit the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) and Insurance Institute for Highway Safety (IIHS) websites. Key safety features include:

        • Standard automated emergency braking with pedestrian detection
        • Standard lane-departure warning with lane-keeping assist
        • Available adaptive cruise control with a lane-centering feature

          Warranty and Maintenance Coverage

          Kia's powertrain warranty sets it apart from most other manufacturers, with most models offering just 5-year/60,000-mile powertrain warranties. The K4 and the Elantrafrom its corporate sibling, Hyundaiare the only compact cars that offer this much standard coverage.

          • Limited warranty covers 5 years or 60,000 miles
          • Powertrain warranty covers 10 years or 100,000 miles
          • No complimentary scheduled maintenance
